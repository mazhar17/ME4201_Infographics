How Net Metering Works

Guideline §2.2, §3.4

A prosumer installs a renewable system on their own premises, uses what they need, and exports the surplus to the grid. A bi-directional meter records import and export separately. Each billing period the bill is based on the net: imported − exported − previous credit. Surplus credits carry forward and are cashed out each quarter.

Billing Units (BU) = Import (I) − Export (E) − Previous Credit (C)

📟 The metering set-up

Guideline Figure 3 — an "indirect" grid connection. The bi-directional Net Meter (M1) is the one that matters for billing.

M1 = Net Meter (bi-directional, billing); M2 = self-consumption meter (optional); M3 = RE/Solar accounting meter (mandatory). The consumer bears the Net Meter cost.

Four things every prosumer should know

🔀

Net, not gross

You pay only for net imported energy; surplus becomes credit.

📅

Quarterly settlement

Left-over credit is settled after Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec — paid at the bulk rate.

✂️

10% + 5% charges

10% of net export is a Distribution System Maintenance Charge; feeders exporting to the national grid also carry a 5% Grid charge.

💳

Fixed charges remain

Even a net exporter still pays demand, meter and other fixed charges.

Two Business Models: CAPEX vs. OPEX

Guideline §2.2, Annex-6

This is the entrepreneurial fork in the road. Under the guideline a net-metering system can be built under two ownership models. One makes you the rooftop owner the investor; the other makes you the developer the investor — installing on someone else's roof and selling them power below the retail tariff. Answer the questions to see which fits.

🔵 CAPEX Model

The consumer self-invests & self-manages
  • 💰 Who pays: the consumer funds the whole system up-front.
  • 🏠 Who owns/operates: the consumer — full control and maintenance.
  • 🎁 Who benefits: the consumer keeps the full net-metering benefit (bill savings + settlement).
  • ⚠️ Who bears risk: the consumer (capital, performance, O&M).
  • 📄 Agreement: bilateral, consumer ↔ distribution utility (Annex-4).
✔ Recommended for you

🟠 OPEX Model

A third-party investor installs & operates
  • 💰 Who pays: the OPEX investor funds and installs — the consumer pays no capital.
  • 🏢 Who owns/operates: the investor; all install, O&M and risk sit with them.
  • 🎁 Who benefits: the consumer buys the solar output at an agreed tariff, generally lower than the retail tariff; the investor earns the margin.
  • 🤝 Agreement: tripartite — consumer + utility + OPEX investor (Annex-4 + OPEX contract Annex-6).
  • ⏳ Terms: fixed term, clear tariff (flat or year-wise), ≥12-month security, and an end-of-term exit plan.
✔ Recommended for you
The startup angle. The OPEX model is a business: as an energy developer you invest in panels on a factory, hospital, mall or campus roof, sell them electricity below the grid tariff, and pocket the difference over a fixed-term contract. The guideline even lets distribution utilities act as OPEX investors and target universities, hospitals, shopping malls and industry. Your engineering skill — sizing, yield, O&M — is the moat.

Eligibility & System Sizing

Guideline §3.1–3.3

Before costing anything, check who qualifies and how big a system the rules allow. The two hard limits: the system's AC output must not exceed the sanctioned load, and for medium/high-voltage consumers it must not exceed 80% of the transformer capacity.

✅ Eligibility checklist (§3.1)

A consumer qualifies for net metering when all of these hold:

  • ✔A legitimate consumer of the local distribution utility, with no outstanding arrears.
  • ✔Electricity produced only from renewable sources.
  • ✔Owner of, or has written permission for, the roof/space (building roof, façade, parking, boundary wall, or suitable land).
  • ✔Consumes the generated energy first, then supplies the surplus to the grid.
  • ✔Meets the capacity & interconnection rules of §3.3 and the utility.

Eligible consumer classes span LT, MT, HT and EHV — residential, commercial, industrial, irrigation, educational, hospitals and more. Legacy rooftop systems can convert with relaxed module-standard requirements.

Sizing rules that bind (§3.3). (B) AC output of the renewable converter ≤ the consumer's sanctioned load. (C) For medium- to extra-high-voltage consumers, capacity ≤ 80% of the transformer's rated capacity. (D) To exceed the contracted load, a separate application goes to the utility and the Grid Stability Committee (Annex-9).

The Money — Billing & Savings

Guideline Annex-5

Here's where net metering pays. Because you're billed on net units against a rising slab tariff, cutting your grid import pushes you down into cheaper slabs — so the saving is bigger than the energy you offset. This calculator reproduces the guideline's residential example (LT-A tariff).

🧾 Bill breakdown

Net billing units = I − E − credit, charged up the LT-A slabs, plus demand charge and 5% VAT.

LT-A slabs (BDT/kWh): 0–75 = 5.26 · 76–200 = 7.20 · 201–300 = 7.59 · 301–400 = 8.02 · 401–600 = 12.67 · >600 = 14.61. Demand charge Tk 42/kW/month.

When the utility pays YOU. If you export more than you import over a quarter, you become a net exporter. Guideline Annex-5(d): Ms. Aditi Chowdhury (DPDC, 50 kW small industry) accumulated 1,500 surplus units in a settlement period; at the DPDC bulk rate of Tk 8.56/unit that's Tk 12,840 — after fixed charges and VAT the utility pays her a net Tk 9,918, via bank, mobile financial service or cheque. Settlement runs every quarter (Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec).
Remember the charges. 10% of net exported energy is retained as the Distribution System Maintenance Charge (you keep 90% as credit); feeders that push power to the national grid also carry a 5% Grid System Maintenance Charge. Settlement credit is paid at the bulk rate, not the retail rate — so self-consumption is worth more than export.

Venture Feasibility — RoI & Payback

Investment decision

Now put on the investor's hat. Whether you're the rooftop owner (CAPEX) or the developer (OPEX), two numbers decide the deal: how many years until the money comes back (Simple Payback Period), and how much it earns over its life (Return on Investment).

Launch Roadmap — From Application to COD

Guideline §3.7, Figure 2

The guideline sets a clear, time-bound process. Apply online at the national portal or on paper, and the utility must move within fixed deadlines. Click each stage to expand what happens and who does it.

1
Collect the form & checklist, then submit online at nem.powerdivision.gov.bd (or hard copy) using the Annex-1 template with all supporting documents. The utility acknowledges receipt; online applicants get SMS/email confirmations and can track status. OPEX first: before applying, the consumer and OPEX investor execute the bilateral OPEX agreement (Annex-6).
2
The utility evaluates the premises, interconnection point and distribution system. If a system modification is needed, you're informed (its cost is borne by the applicant). Otherwise, approval to install the RE system is issued. Permission for the whole submission is issued within 10 working days.
3
Install the renewable system and meters using SREDA-approved equipment (solar modules, inverters per IEC 61727, charge controllers, Net Meter). Complete the Annex-3 Net Metering Checklist and apply for compliance verification. You have up to eight months from the permission letter.
4
The utility inspects and commissions the system for compliance. On success, the Net Energy Metering Agreement is signed (Annex-4; tripartite for OPEX). The execution date is the Commercial Operation Date (COD) — your system is officially earning. The system is registered in the National Renewable Energy Database with a unique SID.
Know your rights. If the utility misses the guideline's timelines, or you disagree with a rejection, you can lodge a complaint or review application with SREDA (online or hard copy) with evidence — a SREDA committee reviews it and its decision is final. Underperformance is monitored too: if annual generation falls 35% below expectation you get advisory guidance; below 50%, a formal remedial request.

Summary

Key takeaways
  • ✔Net metering nets exported against imported energy: Billing Units = Import − Export − Previous Credit; surplus carries forward and is settled quarterly at the bulk rate.
  • ✔Two business models: CAPEX (consumer self-invests, keeps the full benefit) and OPEX (a third-party developer invests and sells power below retail) — the OPEX model is the entrepreneur's play.
  • ✔Sizing limits: AC output ≤ sanctioned load; ≤ 80% of transformer capacity for MV/EHV consumers.
  • ✔Because tariffs rise in slabs, cutting grid import saves more than the energy offset — the guideline's residential example shows a ~70% bill cut; a net exporter can be paid by the utility.
  • ✔Charges: 10% Distribution System Maintenance Charge on net export (90% credited); 5% Grid System Maintenance Charge where feeders export to the national grid; fixed charges always remain.
  • ✔Feasibility hinges on Simple Payback Period and Return on Investment — rooftop solar under net metering typically pays back in a handful of years.
  • ✔Launch: apply (≤5 d) → evaluation (≤7 d) → install (≤8 mo) → inspection & agreement (≤15 d) = Commercial Operation Date, via nem.powerdivision.gov.bd.
