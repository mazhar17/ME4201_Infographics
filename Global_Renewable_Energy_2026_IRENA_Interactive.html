Read it like an engineer: capacity (GW) is what's installed; generation (TWh) is what's actually produced. Both are rising, but capacity is rising faster — because the newest additions are solar and wind, which run fewer hours per year than hydro or thermal plants. A concentrated transition. China (2,258 GW), the United States (466 GW) and India (250 GW) together hold well over half of all renewable capacity. Europe as a whole adds 937 GW. The scale of Asia's lead — 2,895 GW — is larger than Europe, North America and South America combined. Where the panels and turbines get built is now one of the defining questions of global energy.

Renewables' Share of the Grid

Capacity vs. generation

Two numbers, often confused. Renewables are now 49.5% of installed capacity but only 31.7% of electricity generated. Both are climbing — but the gap between them is a lesson in how power plants actually run.

The gap
Why the gap? Capacity factor. A solar farm's nameplate is its power on a bright noon; over a full year it produces perhaps 15–25% of that around the clock. Wind runs ~25–45%. Hydro, nuclear and thermal plants run far more hours. So a grid that is half renewable by capacity is only about a third renewable by energy — the same physics you meet as "load factor" and "capacity factor" in Power Plant Engineering.

Access & Equity

SDG 7 indicators

The transition is real — but deeply unequal. Measured per person, the world installed 544 watts of renewable capacity each by 2024. In Africa, the figure is just 46 watts — about one-twelfth of the global average, for a continent with 18% of humanity.

💵 Clean-energy finance to developing countries (USD bn)

SDG 7.a.1 — international public financial flows for clean energy R&D and renewable production. ~$24.6 bn in 2024.

The equity challenge. Closing the gap between 544 and 46 watts per person is the heart of a just energy transition — and the reason Sustainable Development Goal 7 pairs "affordable and clean energy" with international finance. For engineers, it is also an opportunity: the fastest-growing renewable markets of the coming decades are the ones that are barely on this chart today.

Summary & Sources

Key takeaways
  • ✔World renewable power capacity reached 5,155 GW in 2025 — up 2.55× from 2,021 GW in 2016 — and generated 9,836 TWh of electricity in 2024.
  • ✔Renewables are now 49.5% of world electricity capacity (2025) and 31.7% of generation (2024) — the difference reflects the lower capacity factors of solar and wind.
  • ✔Solar is the world's #1 power technology at 2,397 GW, having grown ~8× since 2016 and overtaken hydropower (1,456 GW) and wind (1,291 GW).
  • ✔The transition is concentrated: Asia holds ~56% of global capacity and China alone ~44% (2,258 GW); the USA (466 GW) and India (250 GW) follow.
  • ✔It is also unequal: the world averages 544 W of renewables per person (2024) but Africa just 46 W — while clean-energy finance to developing countries was ~$24.6 bn in 2024.
