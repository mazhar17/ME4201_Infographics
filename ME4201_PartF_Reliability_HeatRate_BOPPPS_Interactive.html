Reliability & the Forced Outage Rate

Nag (2014), p. 12

Reliability is the ability of a power plant to operate continuously without unexpected interruptions. It's not just technical — it directly shapes operational cost, revenue stability, market competitiveness and reputation. The key metric is the Forced Outage Rate (FOR): the fraction of time a plant is unexpectedly offline. Lower FOR = higher reliability.

Forced Outage Rate = forced outage hoursservice hours + forced outage hours × 100%

Heat Rate & Thermal Efficiency

El-Wakil (1984)

Heat Rate (HR) is the thermodynamic figure of merit for a power plant — the thermal energy input required to generate one unit of electrical energy output. Because HR ∝ 1/efficiency, a lower heat rate means a more efficient plant.

HR = Q̇inPout = Fuel energy flow (kJ/h)Electrical power (kW)
ηth(%) = 3600HR (kJ/kWh) × 100

General form: HR = (ṁf × CV × 3600) / P (kJ/kWh), where ṁf = fuel mass-flow (kg/s), CV = calorific value (kJ/kg). Imperial efficiency uses 3412/HR(Btu/kWh). The factor 3600 converts seconds to hours.

Heating value: HHV vs. LHV — always specify the basis

💧

HHV — Higher Heating Value

Assumes water in the exhaust condenses (latent heat recovered). Standard for coal / steam (ASME).

💨

LHV — Lower Heating Value

Assumes water remains vapour (latent heat lost). Standard for gas turbines (ISO).

Since LHV < HHV, it follows that HRLHV < HRHHV. Always specify the basis when comparing plants — otherwise you can make a plant look artificially efficient. Operational significance (Wood et al., 2013): heat rate sets the marginal cost of generation (λ), benchmarks against design curves, and flags fouling/degradation when it rises suddenly.

Incremental Heat Rate & Economic Dispatch

Nag (2014), p. 17

Incremental Heat Rate (IHR) is the additional fuel energy required to produce one more unit of electrical output at a specific operating point — the marginal efficiency of the plant. It's the number grid operators use to decide which unit generates the next megawatt.

IR = dIdL   I = heat input (kJ/h)  ·  L = electrical output (kW or MW)

Why IHR is the key to economic dispatch

📊

Marginal efficiency

IHR indicates the plant's efficiency at any given load — lower IHR = better fuel efficiency at the margin.

💵

Lower marginal cost

Units with lower IHR have reduced fuel cost per extra unit — preferred in economic load dispatch.

🏆

Competitiveness

Higher IHR raises marginal generation cost, reducing a unit's competitiveness in the merit order.

Case study (from the lecture): for +100 MW over a summer afternoon, three online units show IHR of Fossil Steam 9 500, Nuclear 10 000, Gas Turbine 11 000 kJ/kWh. The operator dispatches the Fossil Steam plant — lowest IHR means the least additional fuel and the most economical choice. Nuclear runs best as inflexible base-load; the gas turbine, though flexible, is least fuel-efficient here.

Why Does IHR Rise at Full Load?

Operational aspect

As a plant approaches its Maximum Continuous Rating (MCR), the incremental heat rate rises sharply — each extra MW needs a disproportionately larger fuel input, and the input-output curve becomes convex. Several thermodynamic mechanisms stack up. The biggest single culprit is parasitic (auxiliary) power — governed by the affinity laws.

🌀 Auxiliary Power & the Affinity Law (P ∝ Q³)

Fans and pumps obey the affinity law: power scales with the cube of flow. Push flow toward full load and parasitic load explodes — punishing net efficiency. Drag the flow.

The stack of penalties at high load

Click each mechanism to expand why it erodes marginal efficiency near MCR.

Fan & pump power ∝ Q³ (affinity laws). Boiler feed pumps and induced-draft fans run flat-out, so a small increase in gross generation demands a massive increase in parasitic load — severely penalising net efficiency.
At high mass flow, steam/gas velocity at the last-stage blade exit rises sharply. Kinetic-energy loss is proportional to velocity squared (V²); this leaving loss dominates at full load and reduces stage efficiency. Higher flow also raises friction losses through blade passages and seals.
High-load operation maximises mass flow, increasing pressure drops (ΔP) across heat exchangers → larger entropy generation (Ṡgen). Heat exchangers operate near saturation and the Log-Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD) drifts from its optimal design point, increasing exergy destruction.
Boiler and HRSG surface areas are fixed; as flow increases, effectiveness (ε) diminishes. Main steam pressure and temperature cannot rise linearly with fuel input due to material limits, so specific work (w = hin − hout) per kg of fluid plateaus.
Approaching creep-rupture limits requires strict temperature control (attemperation); heavy spray attemperation bypasses HP stages or degrades steam quality, cutting cycle efficiency. Maintaining NOₓ compliance at peak load may force non-optimal firing or reduced flame temperatures.
High fuel flow reduces particle residence time → more unburned carbon / hydrocarbon slip. To ensure complete combustion, excess air is raised, which increases the mass of hot gas leaving the stack and carries away useful heat (Qstack ∝ ṁgas · Cp · ΔT).

Cost Reduction Measures

Nag (2014), pp. 12–13

Nag lists six ways to reduce the cost of power generation. They tie the whole module together — reliability (fewer breakdowns), heat rate (higher efficiency) and load factor all reappear here as levers on cost.

a

Equipment selection

Longer life & proper capacities — cuts replacement cost, avoids over/under-sizing, lowers FOR.

b

High load factor

Spreads fixed costs over more energy, reducing cost per unit generated.

c

Efficiency improvement

Lowers heat rate & IHR — less fuel per kWh, directly cutting operating cost.

d

Proper maintenance

Prevents sudden failures & unplanned outages; extends component life.

e

Proper supervision

Early detection of abnormal conditions; fewer human-error breakdowns.

f

Simple plant design

Needs less-skilled personnel — lowers training, labour & error-driven downtime.

Summary

Key takeaways
  • ✔Reliability is critical for stable supply and is measured by the Forced Outage Rate, FOR = forced outage hours ÷ (service hours + forced outage hours). Lower FOR cuts downtime and cost.
  • ✔Heat Rate (HR = Q̇in/Pout) is the thermodynamic figure of merit; lower HR = higher efficiency, with η = 3600/HR (kJ/kWh). Always specify HHV vs LHV.
  • ✔Incremental Heat Rate (IR = dI/dL) is the marginal fuel per extra unit; the lowest-IHR unit is dispatched first in economic dispatch.
  • ✔IHR is lowest at mid-load (≈50–80%) and rises sharply near MCR — driven by auxiliary power (∝ Q³), leaving loss (∝ V²), exergy destruction and stack losses.
  • ✔Cost is reduced by: efficient long-life equipment, high load factor, efficiency improvement, proper maintenance & supervision, and simple design (Nag 2014, pp. 12–13).
