Review — Load Factor: the Core Efficiency Metric

Recap from Part A

Electricity demand fluctuates through the day — in Bangladesh the peak hours run from about 5 pm to 11 pm, and the rest is off-peak. The load factor quantifies this fluctuation, and a higher load factor means lower generation cost, stabler pricing, and less wear on equipment.

🎛 Load Factor Explorer

Drag the sliders — the gauge shows LF = Average Load ÷ Peak Load. Try the lecture example: 300 MW average against a 500 MW peak.

LF = Average LoadMaximum Demand = 300 / 500 = 0.60

📈 A Day on the Grid

Typical daily demand curve — peak window 5 pm–11 pm shaded. The gap between the average line and the peak is what load management tries to close.

Eight Factors that Affect Load Factor

1

Demand variation

Wide gaps between peak and off-peak demand lower LF.

2

Plant type & flexibility

Base-load plants (nuclear, coal) run at higher LF; peaking gas turbines run low.

3

Maintenance & outages

Scheduled maintenance and forced outages cut average output.

4

Fuel availability & cost

Fuel constraints or high fuel cost can limit plant operation.

5

Transmission constraints

Network limits restrict dispatch from the plant, lowering LF.

6

Plant reliability

Higher availability → more operating hours → higher LF.

7

Seasonal variation

Water availability, temperature and demand cycles shift LF.

8

Economic dispatch

Merit-order dispatch by cost and system economics decides who runs.

Load Management in Bangladesh — Four Key Mechanisms

Shifting consumption from high-demand to low-demand periods is the essence of load management (BPDB Annual Report 2024–25):

🏷️

1 · Two-part tariff system

3-phase consumers (low- and high-tension) pay significantly more during peak hours than off-peak — a price signal to shift usage.

📅

2 · Holiday staggering

Coordinated weekly closures of industries, markets and shopping malls across areas spread demand more evenly.

💡

3 · Energy-efficient practices

Energy-efficient bulbs, appliances and pumps reduce overall consumption.

❄️

4 · AC temperature setting

Consumers are encouraged to set air-conditioners at 25 °C, conserving energy and trimming peak demand.

The Cost per kWh

Nag (2014), §1.4
"What factors do you think influence the cost of 1 kWh of electricity produced by a power plant?" Brainstorm — think individually, discuss with your neighbour, then share with the class.

Three Cost Components — Click to Explore

Costs that exist whether or not the plant generates a single kWh — the burden of the investment itself:

DepreciationCapital invested — construction cost including cost of the land

Because fixed costs are constant per year, running the plant more (higher capacity factor) spreads them over more kWh — cutting the fixed cost per unit.

The recurring cost of keeping the plant running:

Salaries & wagesOverhauling of equipmentRepairs incl. spare partsWaterLubricating oilChemicalsMiscellaneous expenses

Usually the largest variable component for thermal plants — coal, gas, LNG or liquid fuel burned per kWh generated. Exposed to global price volatility and exchange-rate fluctuations when the fuel is imported — a theme that returns in the Bangladesh case study below.

Divide the annual total of these three by the kWhnet of electricity sent out per year → the generation cost per kWh.

📉 Why Capacity Factor Rules the Unit Cost

Fixed cost per kWh ∝ 1 / n : the same annual fixed cost divided by fewer generated units. The marker follows the capacity-factor slider on the left.

Bangladesh's fleet ran at n ≈ 36.1% in FY 2024–25 — nearly 3× the fixed cost per kWh compared with the same fleet at 95%. This single fact anchors the case study below.

From plant to consumer: "In order to calculate the electric power cost to consumer, in addition to the production cost (fixed cost, operation and maintenance, and fuel cost), the transmission cost, distribution cost, administration expenses, and return or profit on the investment have to be taken into consideration." — Nag (2014)

Root Cause Analysis — High Energy Cost in Bangladesh

An engineering-systems perspective

High electricity cost is a multi-disciplinary engineering problem: adding capacity alone does not reduce cost, multiple technical, economic and policy factors interact simultaneously, and temporary fixes (e.g. tariff increases) treat the symptom rather than the cause. RCA identifies the fundamental causes that must be addressed for long-term improvement.

🎯 Identified Root Causes

📉

Low asset utilization

Installed generation assets run far below capability (plant factor 36.1%).

🛢️

Imported-fuel dependence

Heavy reliance on imported LNG, coal and petroleum fuels.

📄

Capacity-payment obligations

Contracted generators are paid even when they do not run.

⚙️

High-cost generation mix

Expensive liquid-fuel and reciprocating-engine plants in the fleet.

🏷️

Tariff below supply cost

Bulk tariff Tk 6.80 vs supply cost Tk 12.34 per kWh.

🗺️

Planning & market design

Long-term planning and market-design challenges persist.

6

Engineering-Oriented Corrective Actions

From diagnosis to design

Because the root causes are structural, the remedies are engineering and planning interventions — not just price changes:

ACTION 1

Optimize dispatch to raise the annual plant factor.

ACTION 2

Demand-side management to improve the system load factor.

ACTION 3

Accelerate retirement of inefficient oil-fired plants.

ACTION 4

Expand renewables with energy-storage systems.

ACTION 5

Strengthen domestic natural-gas exploration.

ACTION 6

Least-cost generation planning (life-cycle analysis).

ACTION 7

Real-time data for operational decision-making.

ACTION 8

Gradually align tariffs with actual supply cost while protecting vulnerable consumers.

Takeaway message: Bangladesh has adequate generation capacity — but high energy cost is driven by low utilization, fuel dependence, contractual obligations, losses and the tariff–cost gap. Engineering solutions must focus on optimization, efficiency, diversification and robust planning.

Summary of Today's Session

Five things to remember
  • ✔Power generation cost includes fixed costs, O&M costs, and fuel costs — all contribute to the final unit cost per kWh.
  • ✔Net electricity output (kWhnet) depends on installed capacity, auxiliary consumption, and capacity factor.
  • ✔Capacity factor measures how effectively a plant uses its installed capacity over time.
  • ✔A higher capacity factor lowers per-unit cost and improves plant profitability and sustainability.
  • ✔Engineers can improve capacity factor through maintenance, technology upgrades, hybrid systems, and smart dispatch.
