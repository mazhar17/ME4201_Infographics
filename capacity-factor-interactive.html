Capacity Factor: How Hard Does the Plant Actually Work?

A power plant is a machine bought by the megawatt but paid back by the megawatt-hour. Capacity factor measures what fraction of a plant's theoretical maximum output it really delivers — and in Bangladesh, where installed capacity (~27–28 GW) sits far above the evening peak (~16 GW), low fleet capacity factor is a direct reason electricity costs what it does. Learn the concept, run a plant for a year yourself, and see the economics.

The definition CF = actual energy ÷ (rated capacity × hours)

Picture the plant's whole year as a rectangle: rated capacity tall, 8,760 hours wide. The energy it actually generates is the shaded area inside. Capacity factor is simply the filled fraction of that rectangle.

Capacity factor (plant)

CF = Eactual / (Prated × 8760 h)

Example: a 100 MW plant generating 350,400 MWh in a year → CF = 350,400 / 876,000 = 0.40. It behaved like a 40 MW plant running flat out.

Don't confuse it with load factor

Load factor describes the demand (average ÷ peak load — Module 06). Capacity factor describes the plant. A grid can have a decent load factor while individual plants sit idle — exactly Bangladesh's situation when gas-starved plants can't run.

Related plant ratios

Availability factor = hours the plant could run ÷ total hours (outages excluded). Utilization (use) factor = energy ÷ (capacity × hours actually operated). A plant can be 95% available yet have a 12% capacity factor — nobody dispatched it.

Typical values (illustrative)

Nuclear ≈ 80–90% · large coal ≈ 60–75% · CCGT ≈ 40–60% · wind ≈ 25–35% · solar PV in Bangladesh ≈ 15–19% (the sun sets!) · HFO/diesel peakers ≈ 5–15%. Low CF is not always failure — for a peaker it is the design intent.

What pushes capacity factor up or down?

Some factors are inside the plant fence (availability, efficiency); the biggest ones are outside it (fuel, dispatch, demand, resource). Green raises CF; red drags it down.

▼ Lowers CF

Fuel supply shortage

The classic Bangladesh case: gas-fired plants sit idle or derated because gas allocation falls short. A perfectly healthy plant with no fuel has CF = 0.

▼ Lowers CF

System overcapacity

When installed capacity (~27–28 GW) far exceeds the peak (~16 GW), the fleet-average CF is mathematically forced down — many plants must be idle at any moment.

▼ Lowers CF

Low merit-order position

Expensive plants (HFO/diesel, open-cycle GT) are dispatched last, so they run only at the evening peak. High fuel cost per kWh → few operating hours.

▼ Lowers CF

Resource limits (solar, wind, hydro)

Solar cannot exceed daylight; Bangladesh PV tops out around CF 15–19% however well maintained. Wind depends on wind speed; Kaptai hydro on reservoir inflow and season.

▼ Lowers CF

Outages — planned and forced

Every maintenance day and every breakdown removes hours from the year. Poor maintenance culture converts a few planned days into many forced ones.

▼ Lowers CF

Peaky demand (low load factor)

Module 06's lesson returns: a spiky load curve means lots of capacity exists only for a 3-hour evening peak — capacity that idles the other 21 hours.

▲ Raises CF

High efficiency / low fuel cost

A better heat rate moves the plant up the merit order, so the dispatcher calls it more hours per day. Efficiency buys operating hours.

▲ Raises CF

Reliable fuel contracts & logistics

Firm gas allocation, LNG backup, adequate coal stockyards — fuel security converts installed MW into generated MWh.

▲ Raises CF

Good maintenance & availability

Predictive maintenance, spares management and skilled O&M crews shrink both planned and forced outage hours, expanding the runnable year.

▲ Raises CF

Storage & hybridization

Batteries let a solar plant deliver stored energy after sunset, raising the energy sold per installed MW of the combined facility; flattening demand raises base-plant CF too.

▲ Raises CF

Right-sized capacity planning

Matching new capacity to realistic demand growth (and retiring obsolete plants) keeps the fleet busy instead of paying an ever-larger idle reserve.

▲ Raises CF

Demand growth & interconnection

Growing industrial load, electrification, and cross-border trade give existing plants more hours to sell — more MWh from the same MW.

Utilization efficiency: fixed costs divided by fewer kWh

Why Bangladesh feels this on every bill

Capacity payments to idle plants

Many independent power producers are paid a capacity charge for being available, even at zero generation. With installed capacity far above the peak and gas supply constrained, consumers effectively pay for megawatts that never become megawatt-hours — the purest cost of low utilization efficiency.

Idle capital is expensive capital

A plant financed at commercial interest must repay its loan from kWh sales (or capacity charges). At CF 15%, every kWh carries roughly five times the fixed cost it would carry at CF 75%. The machine doesn't get cheaper by resting.

Utilization vs availability

An efficient system needs both: plants that are available (well-maintained, fuelled) and a system that uses them (right-sized fleet, flat demand, good merit order). High availability with low dispatch is wasted readiness; high demand with poor availability is load-shedding.

The virtuous loop with Module 06

Raising the system load factor (flatter demand) raises the capacity factor of the fleet serving it — the same energy needs fewer installed MW, each working more hours. Flat curve → busy plants → fixed costs spread thin → cheaper electricity per kWh.
