Load Factor: Why a Flatter Curve Means Cheaper Electricity

The grid must be built for the peak but is paid per kWh. Load factor measures how well the expensive machine is actually used — and it quietly sets the price of every unit of electricity. Learn the concept, see what pushes it up or down, then flatten the curve yourself and watch the cost per kWh fall.

The definition LF = average load ÷ peak load

Both curves below deliver the same daily energy (same area under the curve). The grid on the left needs ~15,800 MW of installed plant; the flatter one on the right could serve the same customers with far less. That difference is the load factor.

Load factor

LF = Pavg / Ppeak = Energy (kWh) / [Ppeak × hours]

Always ≤ 1. LF = 1 only for a perfectly flat load. Daily, monthly or annual LF use the same formula over different periods. A typical Bangladesh daily LF is around 0.7–0.8.

Why it matters

Generators, transformers and lines are sized for the peak — capital is spent on kW. Revenue comes from energy — kWh. Low LF means the same costly equipment produces fewer kWh, so each kWh must carry more of the fixed cost.

Related ratios (don't confuse them)

Demand factor = max demand ÷ connected load (per consumer). Diversity factor = sum of individual max demands ÷ system max demand (≥ 1 — peaks don't coincide). Plant capacity factor = energy generated ÷ (installed capacity × hours).

Quick example

A feeder supplies 240,000 kWh in a day with a peak of 15,000 kW. Average = 240,000 / 24 = 10,000 kW, so LF = 10,000 / 15,000 = 0.667. If the peak were shaved to 12,000 kW at the same energy, LF would rise to 0.833.

What pushes load factor up or down?

Load factor is a property of consumer behaviour as much as engineering. Green cards raise LF; red cards drag it down.

▼ Lowers LF

Dominance of residential load

Homes use little power all day, then everything at once after sunset — the classic Bangladesh 19:00–21:00 lighting-and-fan spike. A residential-heavy grid is inherently peaky.

▲ Raises LF

Large share of industrial load

Continuous-process industries (textiles, steel, fertilizer, cement) draw steady power around the clock, filling the night valley and flattening the curve.

▲ Raises LF

Diversity among consumers

Offices peak at noon, homes in the evening, street lighting at night. The less these peaks coincide (higher diversity factor), the smaller the system peak relative to total energy.

▼ Lowers LF

Weather & season

A hot evening switches on millions of fans and ACs simultaneously; the Boro irrigation season (≈ Jan–May) adds huge pumping demand concentrated in certain hours.

▼ Lowers LF

Flat (time-blind) tariffs

If a kWh costs the same at 03:00 and 19:00, consumers have no reason to move any load away from the peak.

▲ Raises LF

Time-of-day pricing & DSM

Cheaper off-peak tariffs, peak-hour awareness campaigns and demand-side management shift flexible load (ironing, pumping, charging) into the valley.

▲ Raises LF

Storage & interconnection

Batteries or pumped storage charge in the valley and discharge at the peak; cross-border interconnection lets non-coincident peaks help each other.

▼ Lowers LF

Single dominant peak driver

When one activity (evening lighting, or festival/Eid patterns, or a heat wave) synchronises millions of consumers, the peak spikes while daily energy barely changes.

The economics: fixed costs divided by fewer kWh

Annual cost of supply = fixed cost (capital recovery, staff, maintenance — proportional to peak kW built) + variable cost (mostly fuel — proportional to kWh). Slide the load factor and watch the fixed slice of each kWh shrink.

Operational efficiency: what a high LF does inside the grid

Better plant efficiency

With a flat load, efficient base plants (CCGT ~60%, modern coal, nuclear) run near their design point. A peaky load forces thermal units into part-load operation, where heat rate worsens — more fuel burned per kWh — and calls expensive open-cycle GT and HFO/diesel peakers into service.

Less start-stop damage & reserve churn

Every daily start of a thermal unit consumes fuel producing nothing, stresses metal with thermal cycling, and shortens maintenance intervals. Higher LF means fewer starts, and less spinning reserve held ready purely for a brief evening spike.

Better use of transmission & lower loss share

Lines, transformers and substations are sized for peak flow. At high LF the same network delivers more kWh, so the network cost per kWh drops; peaky operation also concentrates I²R losses into the high-current peak hours.

Deferred capital investment

If demand-side measures raise LF, the utility can serve demand growth without building new peaking capacity — capital that would sit idle 20+ hours a day. For Bangladesh, every MW of avoided peaker is capital freed for base plants, grid reinforcement, or renewables.
