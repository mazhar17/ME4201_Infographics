Cost of Energy (COE) & Escalating Costs

Introduction · COE

The Cost of Energy (COE) is the average cost to produce one unit of electricity over the entire operational lifetime of a plant. It's the simplest lifetime cost metric — no discounting yet (that comes with LCOE in the next tab).

COE = Total Lifetime CostTotal Lifetime Energy Output = Capital Cost + (Annual O&M × Years)Annual Output × Years

Worked example (COE): a 1 MW wind turbine with $1,200,000 capital and $25,000/yr operating cost generating 2 GWh/yr over 20 years → COE = (1,200,000 + 25,000×20) / (2×20) = 1,700,000 / 40 = $42.5/MWh. Try reproducing it in the calculator.

Key factors behind escalating maintenance costs

🔧

Aging equipment

Wear and tear increases repair frequency and part replacements.

📊

Inflation

Rising cost of materials, labour and spare parts over the plant life.

🖥️

Technological obsolescence

Outdated components need costly custom replacements or upgrades.

⚙️

Operational strain

Small plants run near capacity, accelerating deterioration.

🚚

Supply-chain disruptions

Delays and shortages raise procurement costs for critical parts.

📋

Regulatory changes

New environmental/safety rules impose extra maintenance.

Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE)

Discount rate · time value of money

LCOE is the average cost per unit of electricity over the entire operational life, with all costs and energy discounted to reflect the time value of money. Unlike COE, it captures capital, O&M, fuel and decommissioning on a common basis — so you can fairly compare solar vs. coal vs. gas.

LCOE = Σt=1..N (CapExt + OpExt + Fuelt)(1+r)−tΣt=1..N (Et)(1+r)−t

Discount rate = time value of money. It adjusts for inflation, opportunity cost and project risk over time — an economic tool to evaluate project viability. Lower discount rates reduce LCOE and enhance feasibility.
Interest (Riba) vs. discount rate. Interest (Riba) is any guaranteed return on loans or deposits. If the LCOE discount rate is derived from market interest rates, there can be involvement with Interest (Riba) — so careful selection of the discount rate is essential to avoid unintentional alignment with Interest (Riba).

Return on Investment (RoI)

Profitability

RoI measures overall profitability — the net profit earned over a project's life as a percentage of the money put in. A higher RoI makes a project more attractive to investors.

RoI = Net ProfitInitial Investment × 100   Net Profit = (Annual Revenue × Years) − Total Maintenance − Initial Investment

Simple Payback Period (SPP)

Years to recover capital

The Simple Payback Period is the most intuitive metric for a small-scale entrepreneur: how many years of savings or revenue it takes to recover the initial investment.

SPP = Initial InvestmentAnnual Savings or Revenue

Entrepreneurship & the 10 kW Solar Case Study

Bangladesh · SREDA 2025

Decentralised, small-scale energy projects are increasingly viable in Bangladesh, and mechanical engineers can drive rural development through solar and bioenergy ventures. The tools you've built now come together in one real feasibility study.

🔌

Net Metering (SREDA, 2025)

Under the Net Metering Guideline – 2025, a grid-connected prosumer exports surplus solar energy; a bi-directional meter nets exported against imported kWh, surplus credits carry forward, and any remaining credit is settled quarterly — boosting the return of grid-tied PV.

🌾

Rural development

Off-grid solar replaces diesel for irrigation and small businesses, cutting fuel dependence.

📐

Feasibility tools

RoI and SPP let an entrepreneur judge a project before committing capital.

What's new in the Net Metering Guideline – 2025. The Power Division's updated guideline supports two ownership models — the CAPEX model (the consumer self-invests and keeps the full net-metering benefit, as in this off-grid-style case) and the OPEX model (a third-party investor installs and operates under a tripartite agreement). Credits are accounted in kWh, carried forward, and settled every quarter (after March, June, September, December); leftover credit is paid to the prosumer at the tariff under Clause 3.5 via bank/BEFTN, mobile financial services or cheque. As of the guideline, over 3,763 net-metered rooftop solar systems (≈185 MW) are connected across the six distribution utilities. Source: Net Metering Guideline – 2025, SREDA, Power Division, Ministry of Power, Energy and Mineral Resources, Government of Bangladesh (English translation approved 9 Dec 2025).

Summary

Key takeaways
  • ✔Economic analysis is essential for the feasibility of smaller-capacity plants (10 kW – 10 MW); real-world analysis includes degradation, inflation and maintenance escalation.
  • ✔Cost of Energy (COE) = total lifetime cost ÷ total lifetime energy — the simplest measure of generation cost efficiency.
  • ✔LCOE discounts all costs and energy to reflect the time value of money, enabling fair comparison across technologies; the discount rate must be chosen carefully (Interest / Riba consideration).
  • ✔Return on Investment (RoI) = net profit ÷ initial investment × 100 — indicates overall profitability.
  • ✔Simple Payback Period (SPP) = initial investment ÷ annual savings — estimates how quickly capital is recovered.
  • ✔The 10 kW solar case study (RoI 275%, SPP 5.33 years) shows these tools make small rural energy ventures bankable — aided by the Net Metering Guideline – 2025 (SREDA), which nets exported kWh against imports and settles surplus credit quarterly.
