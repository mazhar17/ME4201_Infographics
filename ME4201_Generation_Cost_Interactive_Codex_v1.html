1Start: why can a kWh cost so much?

A power system may have substantial installed capacity, yet electricity can remain costly when plants are underutilized, fuel is expensive, losses persist, or tariffs stay below supply cost. This module turns the lecture into a set of evidence-based investigations.

Bridge-in: Bangladesh reached 27,414 MW installed generation capacity, while its highest peak generation was 16,603 MW. Why might electricity still be expensive? Form an engineering hypothesis before you continue.

3The cost per kWh

The lecture frames the cost per kWh around fixed costs, O&M costs, fuel costs, and the annual kWhnet of electricity sent out.

▣Fixed costs (FC)

Depreciation and capital invested, i.e. the construction cost of the plant including land cost.

⚙O&M costs

Salaries & wages; overhauling; repairs including spare parts; water; lubricating oil; chemicals; and miscellaneous expenses.

⛽Fuel costs

Fuel cost is a generation-cost component. The output denominator is annual kWhnet sent out.

Electric power cost to a consumer: in addition to production cost, transmission cost, distribution cost, administration expenses, and return or profit on investment must be considered (Nag, 2014).

Live kWhnet calculator

The annual electricity sent out is given by (Nag, 2014):

kWhnet = kWinst × 8760 × (1 − Laux/100) × n

Variables: kWinst is rated (installed) generator output; Laux is auxiliary power consumption (%); n is plant capacity factor; 8760 = 24 × 365 hours per year.

4Load factor and load management

Load Factor (LF) is the ratio of average load to maximum demand. A higher load factor means better utilization of installed capacity and improved economics.

Load Factor (LF) = Average Load / Maximum Demand = Energy Generated (MWh) / [Maximum Demand (MW) × Time (h)]

Live load-factor visualizer

Load-factor result
0.60 (60.0%)

The lecture example: 300 MW / 500 MW = 0.6 (60%). A higher load factor is associated with lower generation costs, stable pricing, and reduced wear on equipment.

Load management mechanisms

Load management reduces or minimizes selected electricity-consuming activities during peak hours by shifting activities from high-demand to low-demand periods.

Select a mechanism to examine how the lecture describes peak-demand management. Peak hour is 5 pm to 11 pm.

6Root Cause Analysis: high energy cost

Root Cause Analysis (RCA) identifies fundamental causes for long-term improvement. The lecture uses a Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagram, system-level engineering analysis, Five Whys, and BPDB Annual Report 2024-25 evidence.

Explore the fishbone

Five Whys drill-down

Why 1Electricity is expensive
Why 2Generation cost is high
Why 3Fuel cost, capacity payments and underutilized plants
Why 4Dependence on imported fuels and inefficient asset utilization
Root causePlanning, market-design and governance limitations
Engineering-oriented corrective actions: optimize dispatch to increase annual plant factor; use demand-side management to improve system load factor; retire inefficient oil-fired plants; expand renewable energy with energy storage; strengthen domestic gas exploration; implement least-cost generation planning; use real-time data; and gradually align tariffs with supply cost while protecting vulnerable consumers.

Key takeaways

Bangladesh RCA: identified root causes

  • Low utilization of installed generation assets.
  • Heavy dependence on imported fuels.
  • Capacity-payment obligations.
  • High-cost generation mix.
  • Electricity tariff below actual supply cost.
  • Long-term planning and market-design challenges.
Session takeawayEngineers can reduce per-unit electricity cost by improving capacity utilization, managing demand, reducing losses, diversifying energy resources, retiring inefficient assets, planning least-cost generation, and using evidence for operational decisions.
